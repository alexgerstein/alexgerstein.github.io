<![CDATA[Alex Gerstein]]>http://alexgerstein.com/Ghost v0.4.2Thu, 10 Jul 2014 07:33:49 GMT60<![CDATA[Projects I've Developed]]>I've always been driven by a desire to create. Learning computer science is, for me, the means to this end. Each new thing I learn--inside or outside the classroom--broadens the range of what I can make. There are few things more satisfying than releasing a new product and seeing others make use of it.

Here are some of the awesome projects I've been able to share with others:

DARTPlan

While looking to learn languages and frameworks that are not covered in my CS courses, I decided to make a webapp that allows Dartmouth students to better plan out their majors. Before the app, students had to look up each course they wanted to take on a website and manually check for scheduling changes and conflicts. My Flask webapp does all of this trivial work by scraping the course pages and providing a simple, user-friendly interface to drag and drop courses during terms when they are offered. Over 1,450 students have already signed-up! The code is currently open-source.

mapd

This iPhone app allowed the Dartmouth community to post and share photos of events around campus. Two of my peers and I went from knowing nothing about Objective-C or XCode to finalizing this social networking app in only six weeks. I personally worked largely on the front-end of the project. Unfortunately, the app is no longer available on the App Store.

SVN Comet


The four-week team project for Software Design and Implementation (COSC 50) required each group to make its own multiplayer physics game, á la Crayon Physics, in C using GTK and the Chipmunk Physics Library. This was the first project I developed with a team. Everyone stepped up tremendously, but the project management experience I gained from leading the team (two of the three other members were busy during crunch time with their senior theses) was invaluable. Early on in the process, I was tasked with the GUI, but as gaps formed in the work, I stepped up to assist with the client-server interaction and physics simulation. I could not have been happier with our final product.

]]>http://alexgerstein.com/projects/20919158-adad-484b-8436-0df860110706Thu, 10 Jul 2014 07:15:00 GMT<![CDATA[Films I've Directed]]>My greatest passion aside from technology is filmmaking. The interest largely grew from my early fascination with Pixar and all of the technical aspects behind the work they do.

After a few courses in 3D animation, I wanted to learn as much as I could about film in general. I now have taken numerous courses in film and, in addition to my Computer Science major, am working towards a Film and Media Studies minor. Here are a couple of the student films I've created:

Why I Joined "The Dartmouth"

While spending my sophomore summer on campus--as sophomores are required (highly encouraged) to do at Dartmouth--I put together a recruitment video for The Dartmouth, our daily newspaper. This is unofficially the first video of the revamped The Dartmouth Multimedia staff. Since putting together a team of directors and editors, we've produced some great work that you can check out on our YouTube Channel.

The Director and Me

I created this film as my final assignment for Filmmaking 1 in Fall 2012. We only had one week to write, film and edit, so the production schedule was tight for everyone. To work around this issue, I wrote a story so that would only require one actor. The main actor actually has a twin brother, so I couldn't convince everyone that only one of them acted in this film.

Snoodles

During the summer leading up to my sophomore year of high school, I was fortunate enough to travel on a National Geographic Student Expedition to Iceland. While there, we were tasked with directing a five-minute short about anything we found interesting. This is a character study I made about a sheep named Snoodles who pestered the patrons of the Cowshed Café in Vogafjosi.

Occupy Digibrain

As the culminating project for my freshman writing course (WRIT 5), I directed my friends in a sci-fi film--part 1984, part Occupy Wall Street--about the dangers of computer-assisted brains. It sounds tacky. It is...but I had a great time putting it together with them.

Laughing through Modernization

I wanted to make my final paper for Japanese Cinema (FILM 42) more accessible to those who have not seen the film, so instead of simply writing a paper on Yasuzo Masumura's underated gem, Giants and Toys (1958), I made a visual essay to accompany it.
